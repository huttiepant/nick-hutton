Key ingredients to being a product-led organisation

It‚Äôs always very easy to look at companies operating poorly day-to-day and complain about how things are run. It‚Äôs much harder to do it in real life, clearly. However, being a product-led, innovative business is something that I think all companies should aspire to. This is a list of some of the key players and what I believe they can do to ensure the business operates in this way.

This is written from a product managers perspective and comes from the view of what an organisation can do to ensure a PM does their best possible work.

*I think building an amazing culture another integral part in ensuring your company has the best chance for success, and there is overlap in this area - being product-led means encouraging failure and learning from it as an example, which is an essential cultural trait for the org. My thinking in culture isn‚Äôt as clearly defined currently so this will focus on what can be done to be product-led.

Key highlights of being product-led

  • The company must be focused on the long-term to a crazy extent;
  • Everything needs to be built around solving customer problems;
  • Extreme focus on a small number of business challenges;
  • All employees really need to be tasked with solving outcomes, rather than focus on outputs;
  • Failing can be a great thing and must be encourages, so long as you learn from it. The speed of learning is of paramount importance;
  • Dealing with unstructured problems is the key to success, as your innovation depends on discovering (and solving) unknown unknowns;

    • How you encourage these behaviours business-wide is beyond the scope of this article, but will be something I document in the future.

    How does each role contribute to this?

    Founder / GM / CEO

    The two main areas of focus for this role to set the business up to be product-led are (1) to define the vision, and ensure that the points in ‚Äòkey highlights‚Äô are acted out in every decision throughout the organisation; and (2) to create and clearly articulate the strategy of how the business will achieve its vision.

    To this point, these are the main outputs I look for from this role:

  • A clear definition of the vision of the business and what they see the world looking like in 15 years;
  • An explicit definition of the customer and what problems they have;
  • An understanding of how to behave in any situation (this isn‚Äôt really an output, it‚Äôs a general feeling that is communicated through the business - it‚Äôs really covered in the culture);
  • 2-3 strategy points that the business is focused on at that time.

    • A note on strategy

    Having 2-3 strategy points sounds easy, it‚Äôs not. The biggest mistake I know of is having 10 strategy points wrapped in vague (but big) MBA-type language. Often, it‚Äôs a ‚Äòstrategy‚Äô like increase revenue. Strategy is how you achieve your goals - not your goals - and it needs to guide what the rest of the organisation works on.     Think of business strategy as a soccer game.     • CPO / Head of Product

    This role needs to not only have incredible knowledge of the business itself, the market and the industry, but they need to be extremely strong in understanding product across industries.

    I don‚Äôt believe PMs are looking up to this person for their incredible technical knowledge of the product, and frequently, PMs may be able to show things in the product that this person hasn‚Äôt seen before.

    It‚Äôs vital for this role to communicate the outcomes that are needed to be achieved, so that the teams below feel empowered to achieve this outcome however they see fit.     This role needs to set product principles, a strong product vision and a coherent product strategy that is ruthlessly prioritised around a few key goals. Without this, you end up with an organisation that is built around optimising components rather than the whole.

    On the culture side, this role must follow the lead of the CEO and ensure that the product team feel comfortable taking risks and failing. One way to do this is to encourage everyone to think of their work in terms of bets.

    Outputs I‚Äôm looking for from this role:

  • Strategic focus for the business and the product over the next year.

  • What guidelines and principles should the individual teams be following when building new products?
  • Does it all make sense from a business and product strategy point of view?

    • Director of Product / VP of Product

    A VP of Product may have 10 or more PMs reporting to them, and their role differs from an individual contributor (IC - a PM or Senior PM) because there is a lot of people management in the role. They will also be on the hook for more strategic level thinking than an IC.

    So a great VP of Product will have good experience as a PM and maybe even a Senior PM, but the role will be very different.

    In this product-led company, the VP of Product must also be a champion of the outcome approach, and be careful to never praise or reward a PM getting excited about an output (a big project they‚Äôve committed to for example).

    For a start, the best people typically want these things:

  • An opportunity for huge impact + personal growth;
  • To work with other great people;
  • To be compensated well (this role must be aware of the market rates and adjust if necessary);
  • No bullshit (politics or red tape).

    • The VP‚Äôs job is to detect if this environment is being set. If it‚Äôs not, they must act quickly to fix it. Good people will frequently leave before they raise their deepest concerns.

    What does a PM need in a product led organisation?

    If as an organisation, you don‚Äôt do all of the above, it‚Äôs going to be very difficult to get the most out of your PMs. The best ones will probably try and do some of the above, but get confused while doing so and not focus on their core job.     But let‚Äôs assume this is all set up nicely, the PMs will have a clear understanding of their role and how they can contribute to the business growing (which is surely what everyone wants). The other things you‚Äôll need to setup as a product-led organisation for the PMs is:

  • Direct access to engineers;

    On top of this, the PM needs exceptional relationship building skills because they need to be inspiring their team of devs and designer, while managing all stakeholders from the marketing team to the head of sales and anyone in between.

    The product-led organisation must help PMs by understanding that it‚Äôs an unstructured problem they‚Äôre working on, and give them space to think. A great product-led organisation will constantly help them in improving their ability of how to think.

    It‚Äôs incredibly easy to criticise these but this article is about how I‚Äôd fix things, so I‚Äôll refrain.

    Who should manage a project then?

  • For a team level project, having engineers collaborate directly is far more beneficial than having the engineers talk to a project manager who talks to another project manager who talks to a developer.   • For gigantic projects spanning multiple teams, there should be a technical program manager who is a developer and they are their own role that spans across multiple teams.

    Meetings

    I would sit on the side of no meetings if there was a debate of traditional meeting structure vs no meetings at all. It‚Äôs not that I‚Äôm strictly anti-meeting, it‚Äôs that I‚Äôm strictly against pointless meetings - and I find most meetings pointless.

    The thing that a lot of people don‚Äôt get with meetings is that it‚Äôs not just the meeting time, it‚Äôs the 30 minutes before and after when you are context switching, or deciding that you can‚Äôt jump into an important piece of work because 30 minutes is not enough time to focus.
